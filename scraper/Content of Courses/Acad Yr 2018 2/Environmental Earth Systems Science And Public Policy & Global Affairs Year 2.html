ES2002 EARTH MATERIALS 4.0 AU
Prerequisite: ES1003
Course will combine theoretical lectures about the formation of crystals and main rocks types followed by lab work where the students will learn about the optical microscope and how to identify main minerals in hand specimen and under the microscope. The students will do a project about a rock of their choice where they will have to identify main components, provide a geological background, and also ideas as why the rock in question might be important for research or society. During the project the student will learn how to organize information and cite literature as in scientific research papers. ES2004 LAYERS & LANDFORMS 4.0 AU
Prerequisite: ES1003
This course introduces students to process geomorphology, sedimentology and stratigraphic principles, and provides tools for the interpretation of sediments and sedimentary rocks and basins. Lectures in weeks 1-2 cover sediments composition, sediment transport and sedimentary structures. Lecture in week 3 introduces the facies approach to sedimentology and geomorphology. The second phase moves to conceptualise the processes of formation, transport and deposition of sediment. During lectures in weeks 5-8 students develop conceptual models for the full range of landscapes and sedimentary environments on earth, from desert dunes to deep seas muds and coral reefs to gravel bed rivers. Lectures from weeks 8-11 introduce the concepts of sequence stratigraphy and sedimentary basin analysis. The lecture series is supplemented by 8 laboratory exercises and 2 field trips the first a half day and the second a 4 day trip to Miri in Malaysia. The field program is a major part of the course both in content and assessment.

ES2202 GLOBAL ENVIRONMENTAL POLITICS & GOVERNANCE 4.0 AU
Prerequisite: ES1001
Environmental issues have entered the centre of international politics and global governance in the recent two decades. This course is to introduce global environmental politics and governance (actors, institutions, mechanisms, norms and power dynamics) to students, and examine the topic by a typology of three categories: global common pool resource, transboundary environmental disputes, and local accumulative environmental change. The course encourages and mentors student?s independent research and critical thinking beyond readings and classroom discussion by assigning them into groups and conduct in-depth case studies, taking them to visit an environmental organization with global outreach and impact, and guiding them to incorporate first-hand observation into systemic learning. Via multiple learning sources and innovative teaching methods, the course aims at opening a compound, dynamic and interdisciplinary field to the students.

Course topics include data collection and measurement, central tendency, descriptive statistics, sampling, inference, hypothesis testing with Chi-square, mean comparison with t-test and ANOVA, simple regression, multiple regressions and logistic regression.
